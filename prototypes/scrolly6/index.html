Method 01: Races won in season

One way to look for the best championship season is to compare how many races the constructors's drivers won in their championship runs.

Looking at just this, we can see that Mercedes's 2014–2016 performance was quite strong, securing the first 3 spots.

In these three years, Mercedes drivers Lewis Hamilton and Nico Rosberg won 51 of the 59 races, finishing first and second in the drivers's cup each year.

While looking at the number of wins in a season is a good place to start, we still can't make a great comparison. In 2016, when Mercedes won 19 of the season's races, there were 21 races. In 2014 and 2015 there were 19 races in each season. If we look at the runs right behind these, McLaren's 1988 run and Ferrari's 2002 and 2004 runs, there were 16, 17, and 18 races in each season respectively.

If we look all the way to the inagural season, 1950, there were only 7 races.

If we want to properly compare the number of wins a constructor had in a season, we have to account for the different number of races in each season.

We can do this by dividing the number of wins in each run by the number of races in that season. For Mercedes's 2016 run it would be:
$$ \frac{wins}{races} = \frac{19}{21} = 0.904761905 $$

Method 03: How close to perfect

Winning is good, having both drivers in the podium is good too, but the best performance for a constructor is to have their drivers finish first and second, a 1-2 finish (read: "one-two finish"). A 1-2 finish means that compared to the competition of a grand prix, the drivers were in top shape, the car had no problems, and the non-racing part of the team (engineers, pit crew, etc.) were in sync and the team had a perfect weekend.
